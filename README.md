# National Database of Childcare Prices: Visualizing the Childcare Affordability Crisis

## Overview

This project uses the National Database of Childcare Prices to examine childcare affordability in the United States from 2008 through 2018. The analysis focuses on trends in childcare costs, household income, unemployment, and labor force participation to understand the financial burden childcare places on American families.

Unlike a traditional data analysis project, this work emphasizes data visualization and visual storytelling. The goal was to transform complex childcare affordability data into accessible visual formats that effectively communicate findings to policymakers, stakeholders, and the general public.

Through an interactive dashboard, presentation, and billboard design, the project demonstrates how data visualization can be used to communicate complex policy issues in a clear, engaging, and actionable way.

---

## Project Objectives

This project seeks to answer the following questions:

- How have childcare costs changed over time?
- How affordable is childcare relative to household income?
- How do childcare costs compare to the federal affordability benchmark?
- How have labor force participation and unemployment trends changed during the same period?
- How can data visualization be used to communicate affordability challenges to different audiences?

---

## Dataset Source

The analysis was conducted using the National Database of Childcare Prices dataset provided by the U.S. Department of Labor Women's Bureau.

Dataset:
https://www.dol.gov/agencies/wb/topics/featured-childcare

Technical Documentation:
https://www.dol.gov/sites/dolgov/files/WB/NationalDatabaseofChildcarePricesTechnicalGuide-2022.pdf

The dataset contains county-level and state-level measures of childcare costs, household income, unemployment rates, and labor force participation from 2008 through 2018. These variables were used to evaluate childcare affordability trends and develop visualizations for policymakers and the general public.

## Data Preparation

The dataset was cleaned and prepared prior to visualization development.

Key preparation steps included:

- Removing variables used only to identify imputed values.
- Using imputed childcare cost values where appropriate.
- Aggregating data by state and year.
- Converting weekly childcare costs into annual estimates for affordability calculations.
- Comparing annual childcare costs to median household income.
- Calculating affordability relative to the federal 7% affordability benchmark.

## Key Findings

### Childcare Costs Increased Consistently

Center-based infant care increased substantially between 2008 and 2018, while family-based childcare costs also experienced steady growth.

### Childcare Exceeds Federal Affordability Guidelines

Families spend approximately 16% to 17% of household income on center-based infant care and approximately 12% to 13% on family-based care.

The federal affordability benchmark established by the Department of Health and Human Services is 7% of household income.

### Families Continue to Depend on Childcare

Despite rising costs, labor force participation among mothers with young children remained relatively stable throughout the study period.

### Childcare Affordability Remains a Policy Challenge

The findings suggest that childcare affordability continues to place substantial financial pressure on families and may limit economic mobility without policy intervention.

## Data Visualization Approach

This project focused on visual communication rather than predictive modeling.

Several visualization principles guided the design:

- Clear visual hierarchy
- Consistent color schemes
- Accessible labeling
- Minimal visual clutter
- Audience-specific messaging
- Interactive exploration where appropriate

Different visualization formats were intentionally selected to communicate the same findings to different audiences.

| Visualization | Audience | Purpose |
|--------------|----------|----------|
| Dashboard | Policymakers & Analysts | Interactive exploration |
| Presentation | Decision Makers | Structured storytelling |
| Billboard | General Public | Awareness and advocacy |

---

## Lessons Learned

This project demonstrated how effective visualization can make complex data more accessible and actionable.

Key takeaways include:

- Different audiences require different visualization approaches.
- Interactive dashboards support exploration and decision-making.
- Presentations help communicate a narrative using data.
- Simple visual designs can effectively communicate important messages to broad audiences.
- Data visualization is not only about displaying information but also about guiding interpretation and understanding.
